Using the PHQ-9 to Screen for Depression:
A Practice-Based Perspective

Based on Levis et al., BMJ, 2019 1

The purpose of this tool is to help you understand what would happen if you were to use the PHQ-9 to screen for depression in your practice:

  • How many of your patients would screen positive versus negative?
  • Of those who screen "positive", how many would be correctly identified and how many would be incorrectly identified?
  • Of those who screen "negative", how many would be correctly ruled out and how many would be missed depression cases?

Answering these questions requires you to estimate the prevalence of undetected and untreated depression in your clinical setting, since screening is done to detect depression that has not already been recognized.

Tips for estimating prevalence in your setting:


  1. Start with a general estimate of major depression prevalence:
    • Patients in primary care: 5 to 10% 2,3
    • Patients in specialty care settings or those with chronic health conditions: 10 to 20% 4
    • Women in pregnancy or postpartum: 5 to 10%5,6

  2. Revise the estimate to exclude the proportion already known to have depression
    • Approximately 50% of patients with depression in primary care have been identified by health care professionals 7

  3. Adjust the estimate if you believe that prevalence is higher or lower in your clinical practice
    • E.g. More or less than 50% of cases have already been identified
